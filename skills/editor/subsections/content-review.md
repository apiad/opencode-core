# Content Review

**Purpose:** Assess factuality, methodology soundness, argument strength, evidence quality, and completeness.

**Rule of thumb:** Ensure accuracy and logic before polishing prose. You can't fix bad content with good writing.

---

## What to Look For

### Factuality & Accuracy
- Are all factual claims verifiable?
- Are statistics and numbers accurate and sourced?
- Are dates, names, and proper nouns correct?
- Is there outdated or potentially obsolete information?
- Are technical specifications or data current?

### Methodology Soundness (for research/analysis)
- Is the methodology clearly stated?
- Is the methodology appropriate for the research question?
- Are limitations acknowledged?
- Is the sample size/data set adequate?
- Are assumptions explicitly stated?

### Argument Strength
- Does the thesis make a clear, defensible claim?
- Are counterarguments addressed?
- Is the argument proportionate to the evidence?
- Are conclusions logically derived from premises?
- Is there any logical fallacy present?

### Evidence Quality
- Is evidence specific and concrete rather than vague?
- Are sources credible and authoritative?
- Is evidence recent enough for the topic?
- Are quotes and citations accurately reproduced?
- Is there sufficient evidence for each major claim?
- Are visualizations (charts, graphs) accurate and labeled correctly?

### Completeness
- Are all aspects of the topic addressed?
- Are prerequisites or background information provided?
- Is the scope clearly defined (and followed)?
- Are key terms defined for the audience?
- Is there anything the reader would need that isn't provided?

---

## What to Flag

### Critical Issues
- **Unsubstantiated claims** — assertions without supporting evidence
- **Factual errors** — verifiable information that is incorrect
- **Logical fallacies** — false cause, ad hominem, straw man, etc.
- **Citation artifacts** — claims that were true in cited source but outdated now
- **Missing source citations** — data, quotes, or claims without attribution

### Evidence Issues
- **False balance** — giving equal weight to opposing views despite evidence disparity
- **Anecdotal overreach** — generalizing from single examples
- **Hasty generalization** — drawing broad conclusions from limited data
- **Weak sources** — using non-authoritative or unreliable sources
- **Quote mining** — selectively quoting to misrepresent source meaning

### Framing Issues
- **Narrow framing** — presenting complex issues as binary
- **Scope creep** — addressing topics beyond the stated scope
- **Scope shrinkage** — avoiding difficult aspects of the topic
- **Missing context** — presenting information without necessary background
- **Appeal to authority** — using authority figures rather than evidence

---

## Checklist

- [ ] All facts are accurate and verifiable
- [ ] Statistics and data are correctly sourced and cited
- [ ] Methodology (if applicable) is sound and appropriate
- [ ] Claims are supported by sufficient, credible evidence
- [ ] Evidence directly supports the arguments made
- [ ] Counterarguments are acknowledged and addressed
- [ ] Limitations and assumptions are stated
- [ ] Scope is clear and consistent throughout
- [ ] All key terms are defined for the target audience
- [ ] Nothing essential is missing; nothing extraneous included
- [ ] Citations follow the required style guide
- [ ] Sources are current and authoritative
- [ ] Visualizations are accurate and properly labeled

---

## What to Fix First

1. Factual errors (highest priority — these undermine everything)
2. Unsubstantiated major claims
3. Logical fallacies in arguments
4. Missing citations for data and quotes
5. Weak or unreliable sources
